Mealtime with little ones can feel like a negotiation. But at J&K, lunch is one of the warmest, most useful parts of our day — and a few of the things we do at the table are easy to bring home.

## What "Community Lunch" looks like

Around noon, everyone gathers for **Community Lunch** together. It's about far more than nutrition. Children help set up and clean their own spot, pass and serve, and practice the small social skills of sitting together — waiting, chatting, and noticing when a friend needs the napkins. Eating as a group turns lunch into a lesson in independence and belonging.

## Why family-style works

When children serve themselves (even a little), they get to listen to their own hunger, try new foods without pressure, and feel trusted. That sense of ownership does more for adventurous eating than any amount of coaxing.

## Gentle ideas for picky eaters

Picky eating is a completely normal phase for ages two to five. These low-stress approaches tend to help:

- **Offer, don't pressure.** Put new foods on the table alongside familiar ones. It can take many calm exposures before a child tries a bite — and that's okay.
- **Split the job.** A helpful rule of thumb: *you* decide **what** and **when** is offered; your **child** decides **whether** and **how much** they eat. It takes the battle out of the table.
- **Bring them into the kitchen.** Kids who wash, stir, or scoop are far more likely to taste the results.
- **Model it.** Eat (and enjoy) the same foods you're hoping they'll try.
- **Keep it calm.** No "three more bites," no dessert bribes. Pressure usually backfires.

## The long game

Some days your child will eat like a champion; some days they'll live on air and crackers. Zoom out — look at the week, not the meal — and keep the table a pleasant place to be.
