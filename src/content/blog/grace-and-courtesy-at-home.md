If you've peeked at our daily schedule, you'll see "grace and courtesy" tucked into our morning **Group Circle Time**. It sounds old-fashioned, but it's one of the most practical things we teach — and something you can easily carry over at home.

## What "grace and courtesy" actually means

In a Montessori setting, social skills aren't left to chance. **Grace** is moving through the world with care — carrying a tray, closing a door softly, waiting for a turn. **Courtesy** is treating people kindly — greeting a friend, saying "excuse me," asking before joining play. We teach these the same way we teach pouring or letters: by showing, practicing, and giving lots of gentle chances to try again.

## The little skills we practice

- **Greetings** — looking at someone, saying hello, and using names.
- **Please, thank you, and "excuse me."**
- **Waiting and turn-taking** — the hardest one, and the most important.
- **Gentle hands and voices** with friends and materials.
- **Cleaning up** — returning a toy to its spot so it's ready for the next child.

## How to reinforce it at home

- **Model it — out loud.** Children copy what they see far more than what they're told. Narrate your own manners: "Excuse me, I need to get by."
- **Keep it short and kind.** A quick, warm reminder beats a lecture every time.
- **Practice when everyone's calm.** Role-play greetings or "asking for a turn" as a game, not in the heat of a meltdown.
- **Notice the wins.** "You waited so patiently for your turn — that was kind."
- **Expect lots of repetition.** These are skills, not switches. Patience is part of the lesson.

## A gentler home, together

None of this is about raising a "polite little adult" — it's about helping your child feel capable and considerate, which makes their world (and yours) calmer. It's a close cousin of [why play-based learning works](/blog/why-play-based-learning-matters/): children learn best by doing, again and again, with a caring grown-up nearby.
