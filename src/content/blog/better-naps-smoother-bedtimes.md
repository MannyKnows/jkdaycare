Few things affect a young child's day — their mood, focus, and appetite — as much as sleep. It's also one of the questions we hear most from parents: *how do I keep naps and bedtime on track?* Here's how rest works in our day, and a few ways to keep it steady at home.

## Rest time at J&K

After our **Community Lunch**, the room slows down for **Nap / Rest Time** (about 12:45 to 2:30). Children settle onto their own mats for deep rest or, if they're not sleepers that day, quiet activities. The point isn't to force sleep — it's to give every child a calm, predictable chance to recharge so the afternoon isn't a struggle.

## Why consistency helps so much

Little bodies love rhythm. When rest happens around the same time, in a similar wind-down way, sleep comes more easily — at daycare *and* at home. Big gaps between weekday and weekend schedules are usually what throw a child off.

## Simple ways to support sleep at home

- **Keep a predictable wind-down.** The same short sequence — bath, books, lights low, cuddle — signals the brain that sleep is coming.
- **Watch the clock, gently.** An overtired child actually fights sleep harder. Aim for consistent nap and bedtime windows.
- **Set the scene** — dim, cool, quiet, and screen-free for the last 30–60 minutes.
- **Let "quiet rest" count.** If your child is dropping the nap, a calm rest with books still restores them (and protects bedtime).

## Navigating the nap transition

Somewhere between three and five, many children start giving up the daytime nap — often unevenly, napping some days and not others. That's completely normal. Watch for earlier bedtimes on no-nap days, and know we'll keep rest time gentle and pressure-free on our end.

## Let's stay in sync

If your child's sleep shifts — a new bedtime, a dropped nap, a rough patch — just let us know at pickup. When home and J&K keep rest consistent together, everyone gets a happier, better-rested little one.
